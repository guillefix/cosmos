<p><strong>Materials science</strong>, also commonly known as materials science and engineering, is the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Science">Science</a> and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Engineering">Engineering</a> of material properties, their design, and uses.</p><p>A <strong>material</strong>, I think, most often refers to a type of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Bulk%20matter">Bulk matter</a> (identified by its composition in terms of phases and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Chemistry">chemical</a> composition). However it may sometimes refer to chemical substances per se, or other non-bulk, but relatively simple, arrangements of matter, as for example, in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Nanotechnology">Nanotechnology</a>. It may even be used for more complex arrangements of matter so that a bulk description is not totally appropriate, such as in &quot;smart materials&quot;, where ideas from <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Complex%20systems">Complex systems</a> may be necessary for their description.</p><p>Materials science needs to describe the specific properties of each material. <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Constitutive%20equations">Constitutive equations</a> play a fundamental role in the theory of these properties.</p><p>The physics of materials is based on <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Condensed%20matter%20physics">Condensed matter physics</a>. If dealing with fluids, it of course uses <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Fluid%20mechanics">Fluid mechanics</a> too.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external"

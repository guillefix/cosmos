also commonly known as materials science and engineering, is the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Science.html">Science</a> and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Bulk%2520matter.html">Bulk matter</a> (identified by its composition in terms of phases and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Chemistry.html">chemical</a> composition). However it may sometimes refer to chemical substances per se, or other non-bulk, but relatively simple, arrangements of matter, as for example, in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Nanotechnology.html">Nanotechnology</a>. It may even be used for more complex arrangements of matter so that a bulk description is not totally appropriate, such as in &quot;smart materials&quot;, where ideas from <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Complex%2520systems.html">Complex systems</a> may be necessary for their description.</p><p>Materials science needs to describe the specific properties of each material. <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Constitutive%2520equations.html">Constitutive equations</a> play a fundamental role in the theory of these properties.</p><p>The physics of materials is based on <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Condensed%2520matter%2520physics.html">Condensed matter physics</a>. If dealing with fluids, it of course uses <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Fluid%2520mechanics.html">Fluid mechanics</a> too.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external"

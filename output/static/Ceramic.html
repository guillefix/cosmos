<p>A <strong>ceramic</strong> is a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="#Rigid">Rigid</a> <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Material">Material</a> that consists of an infinite three-dimensional network of sintered crystalline grains comprising metals bonded to carbon, nitrogen, or oxygen. (<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.degruyter.com/dg/viewarticle.fullcontentlink:pdfeventlink/$002fj$002fpac.2007.79.issue-10$002fpac200779101801$002fpac200779101801.pdf/pac200779101801.pdf?t:ac=j$002fpac.2007.79.issue-10$002fpac200779101801$002fpac200779101801.xml" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">IUPAC</a>)</p><p>Note: The term ceramic generally applies to any class of inorganic, non-metallic product subjected to high temperature during manufacture or use. Or more generally to an inorganic, non-metallic, solid material comprising metal, non-metal or metalloid atoms primarily held in ionic and covalent bonds. Almost always, the term is used to refer to artificially processes <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Material">Material</a>s. Otherwise, one often calls it a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Mineral">Mineral</a></p><p>See also <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceramic" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceramic</a></p>

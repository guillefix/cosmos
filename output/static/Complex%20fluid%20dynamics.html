<p><strong>Complex fluids</strong> are fluids with elements (mostly objects suspended in the fluid), whose dynamics couple with the fluid's dynamics, giving a more complex overall behaviour (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_fluid" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">wiki page</a>). Most important types are <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Dispersion%20(Chemistry)">dispersions</a>, so that they are composed of two coexisting phases. The main types are:</p><ul><li>Solid–liquid (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Suspension">Suspension</a>s)</li><li>solid–gas (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Granular%20material">Granular material</a>s, and some <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Fibrous%20material">Fibrous material</a>s)</li><li>liquid–gas (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Foam">Foam</a>s)</li><li>liquid–liquid (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Emulsion">Emulsion</a>s)</li></ul><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Active%20matter">Active matter</a>, for the interesting and important type of complex fluid, composed of active or driven elements.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://people.maths.ox.ac.uk/dellar/MMP_NonNewt_HT16.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Notes from Paul Dellar's course</a> <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://people.maths.ox.ac.uk/dellar/MMP_Non_Newt_fluids.html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">his website</a></p><p>• Low Reynolds number hydrodynamics, general mathematical results, flow past a sphere. <strong>Stresses due to suspended rigid particles</strong>. However, they can't follow it in its strain deformation. Therefore the strain component of the externally imposed flow finds resistance in the suspended particles (spheres for example), and this resistance means the particles disturb the flow. Because the flow determines the stress tensor, they will affect the stress tensor. In particular, the way the suspended sphere will affect the stress tensor is encoded in the <strong>stresslet</strong>.</p><p>Einstein derived the <strong>Einstein viscosity</strong> through dissipation arguments. Part of these is also found in the book.

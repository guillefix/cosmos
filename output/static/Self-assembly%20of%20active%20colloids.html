This results in a <strong>nonequilibrium analog of ionic systems</strong>, but with the remarkable novel feature of action-reaction symmetry breaking.</p><h3 class=""><u>Effective phoretic interactions</u></h3><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Collective%20behaviour%20of%20active%20colloids">Collective behaviour of active colloids</a> for further derivations of similar effective interactions between active colloids. </p><p>The effective interaction, in the far field regime turns out to be analogous to the Coulomb interaction with generalized charges, that <strong>break action-reaction symmetry</strong>. In particular, we differentiate between the charge that produces the field, α , and the charge that responds to the field, μ .</p><p><u>Model and simulation</u>: There is a highly successful and widely used restricted primitive model (RPM) for charged colloid based on Coulomb interactions augmented with short-range steric repulsion between the particles. A generalization is done to the nonequilibrium active colloids, and the model is analyzed using Brownian dynamics simulations, to explore novel phenomena in this system.</p><p>Periodic boundary conditions are used, and interactions are treated using the minimal image convention (<mark>what is this?</mark>)</p><p><u>Approximations</u></p><p>for simplicity, use a model in which the catalytic activities of the colloids are simplified into net production or consumption of chemicals with given rates. This can manifest itself in the form of frustration that leads to nonequilibrium fluxes. This also mean that the long time behaviour may include limit cycles (<em>oscillatory instability</em> see below).</p><h3 class=""><u>Cluster with <strong>oscillatory instability</strong></u></h3><p>The internal dynamics of quasi-stable (for small perturbations) clusters for the case of two kinds of particles (A and B) can be analyzed using d'Alembert's principle (see their Appendix). A Hopf bifurcation can take place (where the parameters are the charges of the two kinds of particles), so that in a certain regime a stable limit cycle forms. This is the oscillatory instability. This is demonstrated in the A<sub>4</sub>B<sub>8</sub> colloidal molecule.</p><p><mark>What symmetry makes the second harmonic absent?</mark> Probably some dynamical symmetry</p><h3 class=""><u>Cluster with <strong>run-and-tumble behaviour</strong></u></h3><p>In the AB<sub>3</sub> molecule one finds that in many parameter regimes, there are two stable configurations, and the system stochastically jumps between the two. One of the configurations has the B colloids symmetrically placed around the A, while in the other they are asymmetrical, causing (due to the asymmetry of the forces of the colloids in the fluid) a net self-propelling velocity. </p><p>The motion of the internal degrees of freedom is again derived using d'Alembert's principle. There is an angle variable which is cyclic, due to rotational invariance, and gives a conservation law. The other two angles follow a set of coupled ODEs which have equilibria corresponding to the stable configurations.</p><p>By simplifying the dynamics to the line where the two angles are equal (because both equilibria lie on it), one can obtain a single-variable <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Langevin%20equation">Langevin equation</a> and a corresponding <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Fokker-Planck%20equation">Fokker-Planck equation</a> to study the probability distribution of the system, which can be used to find, for instance, how much time is spent on run vs tumble behaviour.

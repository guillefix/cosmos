<p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Enzyme%20inhibition">Enzyme inhibition</a></p><h2 class=""><u>Negative feedforward of feedback</u></h2><p>There are two major devices for achieving negative feedforward or feedback. One way is to prevent the relevant enzymes from being able to perform-that is, to &quot;clog up&quot; their active sites. This is called inhibition. The other way is to prevent the relevant enzymes from ever being manufactured! This is called repression. Conceptually, inhibition is simple: you just block up the active site of the first enzyme in the assembly line, and the whole process of synthesis gets stopped dead. </p><h2 class=""><u>Positive feedback and feedforward</u></h2><p>What about positive feedback and feedforward? Here again, there are two options: (1) unclog the clogged enzymes (<strong>un-inhibit</strong>), or (2) stop the repression of the relevant operon. (Notice how <b>nature seems to love double-negations! </b> Probably there is some very deep reason for this.) </p><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Gene%20expression%20regulation">Gene expression regulation</a> too </p>

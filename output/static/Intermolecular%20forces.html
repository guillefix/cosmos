<span class="tc-drop-down tc-reveal" hidden="true"></span> </span> </div> </div> <div class="tc-tiddler-body tc-reveal"><p>Intermolecular forces are usually composed of a repulsive and an attractive part:</p><ul><li>The <strong>repulsive part</strong> is essentially quantum mechanical (in particular, the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="Pauli%2520exclusion%2520principle.html">Pauli exclusion principle</a> plays a key role). However, often we approximate it by a hard-sphere infinitely steep potential, because the real potential is much steeper than other potentials in most problems.</li><li>The <strong>attractive part</strong> shows more variability, but it almost invariably is of electrostatic origin.</li></ul><p>If the potential indeed has an attractive component (the repulsive one always exists), then the potential will present a minimum, corresponding to an equilibrium state, known as a <strong>bond</strong>. The depth of the potential minimum (relative to the thermal energy, <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><msub><mi>k</mi><mi>B</mi></msub><mi>T</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">k_B T</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.69444em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.84444em;vertical-align:-0.15em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord"><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right:0.03148em;">k</span><span class="vlist"><span style="top:0.15em;margin-right:0.05em;margin-left:-0.03148em;"><span class="fontsize-ensurer reset-size5 size5"><span style="font-size:0em;">​</span></span><span class="reset-textstyle scriptstyle cramped"><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right:0.05017em;">B</span></span></span><span class="baseline-fix"><span class="fontsize-ensurer reset-size5 size5"><span style="font-size:0em;">​</span></span>​</span></span></span><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right:0.13889em;">T</span></span></span></span></span>) determines the <em>strength</em>, or <em>stiffness</em> of the bond. One often makes a distinction between:</p><ul><li><strong>Chemical bonds</strong> or permanent bonds (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Chemical%2520bond.html">here</a>) are those with bond energy is much larger than the thermal energy.</li><li><strong>Physical bonds</strong> or temporary bonds are those with bond energy comparable to, or just a bit bigger than the thermal energy.</li></ul><p><u>Common intermolecular forces</u></p><ul><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Van%2520der%2520Waals%2520force.html">Van der Waals force</a>s. </li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Hydrogen%2520bond.html">Hydrogen bond</a>s. </li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Hydrophobic%2520interaction.html">Hydrophobic interaction</a>. </li></ul><hr><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yFq4EieGWF0" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">this video</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yFq4EieGWF0#t=6m" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">examples</a></p></div> </div> </p> </section> </body> </html>

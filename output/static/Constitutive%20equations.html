tc-reveal"><p>(<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constitutive_equation" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">wiki</a>) In physics and engineering, a <strong>constitutive equation</strong> or <strong>constitutive relation</strong> is a relation between two physical quantities (especially kinetic quantities as related to kinematic quantities) that is specific to a material or substance, and approximates the response of that material to external stimuli, usually as applied fields or forces.</p><p>They are often just <u>phenomenological</u>, because bulk material, or a sufficiently large amount of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Condensed%2520matter%2520physics.html">condensed matter</a>, is a very complex system, made of many interacting particles. However, they should be, in principle, and sometimes are in practice, <u>derivable from principles of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Statistical%2520physics.html">Statistical physics</a>, and often <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Non-equilibrium%2520statistical%2520physics.html">Non-equilibrium statistical physics</a></u>. </p><p>Those constitutive relations that are used in the description of the autonomous <u>time-evolution</u> of a system often need <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Non-equilibrium%2520statistical%2520physics.html">Non-equilibrium statistical physics</a>, as systems that macroscopically (i.e. the relevant averaged quantities) evolve in time are by definition out of equilibrium.</p><p>Constitutive relations for driven systems, that are in <u>quasi-equilibrium</u>, should be derivable from <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Equilibrium%2520statistical%2520physics.html">Equilibrium statistical physics</a>.</p><h2 class=""><u>Constitutive equations in non-equilibrium</u></h2><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Kinetic%2520theory.html">Kinetic theory</a> offers a foundation to derive constitutive equations from the microscopic details of the material. However, derivations are often hard, and give only qualitatively correct answers (more precisely, the answers are often correct up to an order <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mn>1</mn></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">1</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.64444em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.64444em;vertical-align:0em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathrm">1</span></span></span></span></span> constant, because of approximations).</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="Non-equilibrium%2520thermodynamics.html">Non-equilibrium thermodynamics</a> is often based itself on more or less phenomenological principles. However, these principles can be very useful for deriving constitutive relations for large classes of systems.</p><p>An example, of one of these principles is the principle that <em>the rate of entropy production be maximal</em>. This is used in <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://ncmm.karlin.mff.cuni.cz/preprints/11289092919pr19.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">this paper</a> to derive the Allen-Cahn equations used to describe the evolution of phase fields (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Phase%2520transition.html">Phase transition</a>).</p><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://rspa.royalsocietypublishing.org/content/royprsa/460/2042/631.full.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">On thermomechanical restrictions of continua</a> for the paper proposing the above principle.</p><p><em>I'm sure there are other approaches, and I should learn more about <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Non-equilibrium%2520statistical%2520physics.html">Non-equilibrium statistical physics</a> in general, to learn, and organize these important ideas better.</em></p></div> </div> </p> </section> </body> </html>

<p>A <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colloid" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">colloid</a> is most often used to refer to either:</p><ul><li>a <strong>colloidal dispersion</strong>, or</li><li>a <strong>colloidal particle</strong></li></ul><p>Colloidal: State of subdivision such that the molecules or polymolecular particles dispersed in a medium have at least one dimension between approximately 1 nm and 1 Î¼m, or that in a system discontinuities are found at distances of that order. (IUPAC)</p><p>Colloid: Short synonym for colloidal system. (IUPAC)</p><p>A colloidal dispersion is a system in which particles of colloidal size of any nature (e.g. solid, liquid or gas) are <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Dispersion%20(Chemistry)">dispersed</a> in a continuous phase of a different composition (or state). The &quot;name dispersed&quot; phase for the particles should be used only if they have essentially the properties of a bulk phase of the same composition.</p><p>(<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://goldbook.iupac.org/C01174.html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">http://goldbook.iupac.org/C01174.html</a>)</p><h2 class=""><u><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Colloid%20physics">Colloid physics</a></u></h2><p>Branch of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Physics">Physics</a> dealing with physical properties of colloidal systems (i.e. motion, forces, etc. at the scale of the colloidal system).</p><p>See book by Hunter - Foundations of colloid science </p>

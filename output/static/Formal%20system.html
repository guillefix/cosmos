<span class="tc-drop-down tc-reveal" hidden="true"></span> </span> </div> </div> <div class="tc-tiddler-body tc-reveal"><p>A <strong>Formal system</strong> is a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Set.html">Set</a> of rules you can apply to a set of objects.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Formal%2520language.html">Formal language</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_grammar" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_grammar</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Formal%2520systems%2520and%2520semantics.html">Formal systems and semantics</a></p><p>MU-puzzle</p><h2 class=""><u>Rewrite systems</u></h2><p>The most common type of formal system. (Are there other types actually?)</p><p><u>(Abstract) Rewrite systems</u></p><p>A set of objects, and a binary operation, <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mo>→</mo></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\rightarrow</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.36687em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.36687em;vertical-align:0em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mrel">→</span></span></span></span></span>, that tells us how we are allowed to transforms expressions. If these rules act on <em>terms</em> out of which an expression can be built, then this is a term rewrite system.</p><p>They are non-deterministic Markov algorithms, and they are Turing complete. They are related to normal forms, lambda calculus, and combinatory logic</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.cs.tau.ac.il/~nachum/papers/survey-draft.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">http://www.cs.tau.ac.il/~nachum/papers/survey-draft.pdf</a></p><p>Initial strings in a formal theorem are called <em>axioms</em>, while any string producible by its rules are konwn as <em>theorem</em>s.</p><p>The rules themselves are knwon as production rules or rules of inference.</p><p>A series of production rules that leads to a theorem is known as a derivation of that theorem.</p><h3 class=""><u>Decision procedure and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="Decidable.html">Decidability</a></u></h3><p>A <strong>decision procedure</strong> is a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="Computable.html">Computable</a> procedure (i.e. one that is guaranteed to finish in a finite amount of time) that determines whether a theorem will be reached by the production rules of the formal system, or not.</p><p>A theorem is <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="Decidable.html">Decidable</a> if it has a decision procedure for it.</p><p>The axioms must always be decidable. Their decision procedure is often in the form of an <em>axiom schema</em>, with substitutable variables which are clearly defined.</p><p>Formal systems with only <em>lengthening</em> rules always have a decision procedure for all strings. See page 48 of GEB.</p><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Computability%2520theory.html">Computability theory</a></p></div> </div> </p> </section> </body> </html>

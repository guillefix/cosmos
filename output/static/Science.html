<p>The knowledge, methods, and everything else regarding the <em>understanding</em> of the Cosmos. This includes essentially structures based on logic (and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Mathematics">Mathematics</a>, in general) that must match what is <u>observed</u> in the <strong>Cosmos</strong>. See <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OL6-x0modwY" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">The Scientific Method-Richard Feynman</a>, and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Philosophy%20of%20science">Philosophy of science</a></p><p>&quot;Science is the attempt to set in order the facts of experience.&quot; ~ <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="#Buckminster%20Fuller">Buckminster Fuller</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ohyai6GIRZg" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Map of science</a></p><h3 class=""><strong>Formal sciences</strong> and <strong>philosophy</strong></h3><p>Lay the concrete foundation for the rest of the sciences, by looking at fundamental structures and ideas. From the more theoretical to the more applied:</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Philosophy%20of%20science">Philosophy of science</a> -&gt; <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Mathematics">Mathematics</a> -&gt; <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Theoretical%20computer%20science">Theoretical computer science</a> -&gt; <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Mathematical%20methods">Mathematical methods</a> and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Scientific%20computing">Scientific computing</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portal:Contents/Mathematics_and_logic" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Portal:Contents/Mathematics and logic</a></p><h3 class=""><strong>Natural science</strong></h3><p>Natural science is often defined as the part of science studying natural phenomena (that is, those not cause by <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Humanities">Humans</a>). These are in some sense, the <u>foundational sciences</u>, as everything (including Humanity) is ultimately part of Nature (the Physical world). </p><ul><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outline_of_physical_science" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Physical science</a>, related to non-living beings, including <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Physics">Physics</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Chemistry">Chemistry</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Materials%20science">Materials science</a>, and, to some extent, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Earth%20science">Earth science</a> and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Space%20science">Space science</a></li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Life%20sciences">Life sciences</a>, including <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Biology">Biology</a>, and related ones.</li></ul><p>Roughly, we can categorize the natural sciences, in order of the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Complexity%20theory">complexity</a> of the studied systems, forming a sort of hierarchy, or emergent new phenomena:</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Physics">Physics</a> -&gt; <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Chemistry">Chemistry</a> -&gt; <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Biology">Biology</a> -&gt; <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Cognitive%20science">Cognitive science</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portal:Contents/Natural_and_physical_sciences" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Portal:Contents/Natural and physical sciences</a></p><h3 class=""><strong>Systems sciences</strong></h3><p>Systems sciences studies very complex natural phenomena, as well as human phenomena (which are of course, a result of natural phenomena, but often of the highest complexity we know). It is the application and integration of the more reductionist ideas of the foundational sciences to larger systems.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="#Systems%20Science">Systems Science</a>s are the highest level of complexity, looking at parts of the Cosmos made of many parts interacting in complex ways.</p><p>Some of the most important ones are <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Social%20sciences">Social sciences</a>, which look at societies (large collections of complex agents).</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portal:Contents/Society_and_social_sciences" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Portal:Contents/Society and social sciences</a></p><hr><p>The distinctions above are fuzzy, and a bit ambiguous. This is partially because of the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#History%20of%20science">History of science</a> being very complex, and with conflicting ideas of how science should be organized.</p><p>However, as can be seen from above, my preferred way of organizing it is an approximate hierarchy of complexity: from simple (reductionist) laws to complex systems.</p><hr><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Portal/Directory/Science_and_mathematics" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Wikipedia:Portal/Directory/Science and mathematics</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xRjEPwL8Lxk" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Thaumaturgy in the Age of Science by Prof. V.

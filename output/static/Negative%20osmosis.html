guillefix </a> 4th November 2016 at 9:43am </div> </div> <div class=" tc-reveal"> <div class="tc-tags-wrapper"></div> </div> <div class="tc-tiddler-body tc-reveal"><p>When a flow of liquid occurs through a membrane from a more concentrated solution to a more dilute solution, it is designated as negative osmosis. Compare with (positive) <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Osmosis.html">Osmosis</a></p><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Physical%2520mechanisms%2520of%2520osmosis.html">Physical mechanisms of osmosis</a></p><h3 class="">See here: <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/265752/can-osmosis-go-the-other-way/265879#265879" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">http://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/265752/can-osmosis-go-the-other-way/265879#265879</a></h3><h3 class=""><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1334591/pdf/biophysj00323-0049.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">MECHANISM OF OSMOTIC FLOW IN POROUS MEMBRANES</a></h3><p>Many standard theoretical calculations of equilibrium osmotic pressure work under very ideal/simplifying assumptions; like the lattice model in Physical biology of the cell book.</p><p>Similarly treatments of osmotic flow are often simplified see for instance <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/037673889500102I" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">The solution-diffusion model: a review</a>.</p><p>Real life application of osmotic flow need more complete descriptions, which include parameters, which are often measured, mainly the <strong>reflection coefficient</strong>. Results can often differ (often just quantiatively) from more naive thermodynamic treatments.</p><p>When you try to find these parameters theoretically is when you get into the hard part, as a microscopic model is needed, whether based on kinetics, or hydrodynamics. This is where the richness of real-life phenomena comes to light.</p><p>Careful theoretical treatment has found negative reflection coefficients to be possible: <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1334591/pdf/biophysj00323-0049.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">MECHANISM OF OSMOTIC FLOW IN POROUS MEMBRANES</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0927775703001444" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Diffusioosmosis of nonelectrolyte solutions in a fibrous medium</a> However, I think they are only possible for non-perfectly-semipermeable membranes! See below. Actually Anderson's paper agrees! Note that in his figure 5, negative reflection coefficient is found only when the solute is smaller than the pore!</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/0022519381903210" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Configurational effect on the reflection coefficient for rigid solutes in capillary pores</a></p><p>In the case of osmosis of electrolytes, there's more studies: <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://science.sciencemag.org/content/161/3836/70" rel="noopener noreferrer"

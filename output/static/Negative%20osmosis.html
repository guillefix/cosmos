<p>When a flow of liquid occurs through a membrane from a more concentrated solution to a more dilute solution, it is designated as negative osmosis. Compare with (positive) <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Osmosis">Osmosis</a></p><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Physical%20mechanisms%20of%20osmosis">Physical mechanisms of osmosis</a></p><h3 class="">See here: <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/265752/can-osmosis-go-the-other-way/265879#265879" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">http://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/265752/can-osmosis-go-the-other-way/265879#265879</a></h3><h3 class=""><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1334591/pdf/biophysj00323-0049.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">MECHANISM OF OSMOTIC FLOW IN POROUS MEMBRANES</a></h3><p>Many standard theoretical calculations of equilibrium osmotic pressure work under very ideal/simplifying assumptions; like the lattice model in Physical biology of the cell book.</p><p>Similarly treatments of osmotic flow are often simplified see for instance <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/037673889500102I" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">The solution-diffusion model: a review</a>.</p><p>Real life application of osmotic flow need more complete descriptions, which include parameters, which are often measured, mainly the <strong>reflection coefficient</strong>.

<p>The <strong>spindle</strong>, or <strong>spindle apparatus</strong> is an structure that segregates chromosomes during cell division, and is formed by <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Microtubule">Microtubule</a>s, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Molecular%20motors">Molecular motors</a>, and hundreds of other proteins. The spindle <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Spindle%20self-organization">self-organizes</a> during division process.</p><p>(<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spindle_apparatus" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spindle_apparatus</a>)</p><p><img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/Kinetochore.jpg"></p><p>For the frog <em>Xenopus laevis</em>, spindles are on average ~45 microns long, and ~30 microns wide. Microtubules in these spindles have an average length of ~7 microns (<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://people.seas.harvard.edu/~jbrugues/Home_files/nucleation_transport_cell_2012.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ref</a>) and are at a density of ~50-100 microtubules/<span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>μ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.43056em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.19444em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathit">μ</span></span></span></span></span>m^2, implying that there are ~100,000 (<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/1238403" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ref 1</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC2141625/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ref 2</a>). Microtubules are <u>polar polymers</u> whose minus ends are relatively static and whose plus ends polymerize at a speed of ~10-20 <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>μ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\mu</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.43056em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.625em;vertical-align:-0.19444em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathit">μ</span></span></span></span></span>m/min (<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC379274/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ref</a>). There is no appreciable rate of rescues in these spindles <mark>?</mark> (<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://people.seas.harvard.edu/~jbrugues/Home_files/nucleation_transport_cell_2012.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">ref</a>), and the half-life of these microtubules is ~16s, much shorter than the typical lifetime of a spindle – which can exist for several hours.

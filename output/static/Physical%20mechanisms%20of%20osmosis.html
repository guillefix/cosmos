<p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Physics">Physical</a> mechanisms of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Osmosis">Osmosis</a></p><h2 class=""><u>Macroscopic/thermodynamic description</u></h2><p>Based on <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Chemical%20potential">Chemical potential</a>s, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Solution%20(Chemistry)">Solution (Chemistry)</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/037673889500102I" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">The solution-diffusion model: a review</a></p><h2 class=""><u>Microscopic mechanism</u></h2><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC1334591/pdf/biophysj00323-0049.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">MECHANISM OF OSMOTIC FLOW IN POROUS MEMBRANES</a></p><p>The standard chemical potential explanation still holds as part of the mechanism. See <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/212183/physic-explanation-to-osmosis?rq=1" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">here</a>. The energy comes from the expansion of the solute (which works like an ideal gas), just like in <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.physicsforums.com/threads/work-done-in-adiabatic-quasistatic-compression.138448/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">quasistatic adiabatic expansion</a>.</p><p>However, the boundary layer given by a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Diffusio-osmosis">Diffusio-osmotic</a> effect, enhances the checmical potential difference at the pore increasing the osmotic pressure. The extra work done in the process, I think, ultimately comes from the fact that the potential energy near the wall is lowered as the solute concentration decreases during the process.</p><p>When membrane is semi-permeable (as in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Osmosis">Osmosis</a> proper), then I think that the main effect would be an excluded volume effect (this appears to be indeed the case, at least for purely semi-impermeable membranes see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Negative%20osmosis">Negative osmosis</a>), giving rise to an effective repulsive potential, like that in Nelson's Biological physics book, or those that appear in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Diffusio-osmosis">Diffusio-osmosis</a>, or <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Diffusiophoresis">Diffusiophoresis</a>.</p><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Interfacial%20forces">Interfacial forces</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://ajpregu.physiology.org/content/256/4/R801.short" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Molecular mechanisms of osmosis</a> <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0085253815326570" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Mechanism of osmosis</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://advan.physiology.org/content/27/1/15.full.pdf+html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">OSMOSIS: A MACROSCOPIC PHENOMENON, A MICROSCOPIC VIEW</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://sci-hub.cc/10.1016/j.tplants.2012.12.001" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Osmosis is not driven by water dilution</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://phys.org/news/2013-04-osmosis-wrong.html#jCp" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">here too</a>. See Nelson's Biological physics book for more details</p><p>The mechanism is based on the wall repelling the solute molecules.

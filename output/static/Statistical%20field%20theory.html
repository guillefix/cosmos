<p>A <strong>statistical field</strong> is often derived by averaging microscopic physics over mesoscopic lengthscales (in a particular way called, <u>coarse graining</u>). This results in a <u>free enegy</u>, <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>F</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">F</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.68333em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.68333em;vertical-align:0em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right:0.13889em;">F</span></span></span></span></span>, which (when exponentiated) gives the weight factor over which we integrate to get the <u>partition function</u>, <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>Z</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Z</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.68333em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.68333em;vertical-align:0em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right:0.07153em;">Z</span></span></span></span></span>. As the averaging gives a (macroscopic) field (as an approximation to a lattice average), the integral for <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>Z</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">Z</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.68333em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.68333em;vertical-align:0em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right:0.07153em;">Z</span></span></span></span></span> is a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="#Functional%20Integral">Functional Integral</a>, expressable as a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="#Path%20Integral">Path Integral</a>.</p><h3 class=""><u>Origin of Universality (Why do many field theories look like each other?)</u></h3><p>This free energy can be written as a power series in the field. It turns out that only a few terms (<small>the renormalizable ones, and maybe a few non-renormalizable ones</small>) contribute for a given precission of interest (this is understood via the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-missing" href="#Renormalization%20Group">Renormalization Group</a>).</p><p>Thus, the only thing that fundamentally differentiates one theory/model from another are the <u>symmetries</u> of the field, which determine which terms can appear in the free energy. <u>Dimensionality</u> and <u>transformation properties</u> of the field (whether it is a scalar, a vector, a spinor, ...) also play a role.</p><p>The microphysics only enters through the <u>parameters</u> of the theory. But as these are often few, they can be and most often are determined experimentally. For this reason statistical field theories are often referred to as <u>phenomenological</u>.</p><p><small>Similar considerations apply in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Quantum%20field%20theory">Quantum field theory</a></small></p><h2 class="">The Landau-Ginzbyurg Hamiltonian</h2><p><u>Assumptions</u>:</p><ul><li>Locality and uniformity</li><li>Isotropy</li><li>Stability</li></ul>

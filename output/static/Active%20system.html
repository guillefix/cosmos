<p>A system with constituents that are able to <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Energy%20production">produce their own energy</a>. For instance, they are often <strong>self-propelling</strong>. See also the <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_matter" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">wiki article</a>. Due to the energy consumption, these systems are intrinsically <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Non-equilibrium%20statistical%20physics">out of thermal equilibrium</a>.</p><p>If the system is made of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Bulk%20matter">bulk matter</a>, it's called <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Active%20matter">Active matter</a>.</p><p>Examples of active systems are schools of fish, flocks of birds, bacteria, artificial self-propelled particles, and self-organising bio-polymers such as microtubules and actin, both of which are part of the cytoskeleton of living cells. </p><p>Dry active systems</p><hr><p>A prominent example of active system are <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Active%20colloid">Active colloid</a>s</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Biophysics">Biophysics</a> (biological systems are active systems).</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://journals.aps.org/pre/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevE.58.4828" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Flocks, herds, and schools: A quantitative theory of flocking</a>.

function posesses an asymptotic approximation in terms of an asymptotic sequence, then that approximation is unique for that particular sequence.</p><p>Note that the uniqueness is for a given sequence. A single function may have many asypmtotic approximations, each in terms of a different sequence.</p><p>Note also that the uniqueness is for a given function: two functions may share the same asymptotic approximation, because they differ by a quantity smaller than the last term included. Two functions sharing the same asymptotic power series, as above, can only differ by a quantity which is not analytic, because two analytic functions with the same power series are identical.</p><p>Asymptotic approximations can be naively added, subtracted, multiplied or divided, resulting in the correct asymptotic expression for the sum, difference, product or quotient, perhaps based on an enlarged asymptotic sequence.</p><p>One asymptotic series can be substituted into another, although care is needed with exponentials.</p><p>Asymptotic expansions can be integrated term by term with respect to <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>ϵ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\epsilon</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.43056em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.43056em;vertical-align:0em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathit">ϵ</span></span></span></span></span> resulting in the correct asymptotic expansion of the integral. However, in general they may not be differentiated with safety, i.e., when differentiating there is always the worry that neglected higher-order terms suddenly become important.</p><h2 class=""><u>Numerical use of divergent series</u></h2><p><strong>Optimal truncation</strong>: Truncating at the smallest term is known as optimal truncation.</p><h2 class=""><u>Parametric expansions</u></h2><p>So far we have been considering functions of a single variable as that variable tends to zero.

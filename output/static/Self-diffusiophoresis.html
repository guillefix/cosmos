tc-reveal"><p>In <strong>Self-diffusiophoresis</strong> (a kind of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Self-propelled%2520particle.html">self-propulsion</a>), a particle itself produces the compound it interacts with, through <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Diffusiophoresis.html">Diffusiophoresis</a>, causing it to move.</p><p>Self-phoretic particle. Creates something that it then attracts or repells, and that something then pushes the surrounding fluid (creating a slip velocity). The particle is then indirectly pushing on the fluid. Same kind of indirect propulsion as ionocrafts!</p><p>Another analogy for the symmetric catalytic <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Active%2520colloid.html">Active colloid</a>s, in the limit that particles of type B are attracted to A, but A is not attracted to B (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="file:///home/guillefix/Dropbox/Oxford/MMathPhys/topics%20in%20soft%20and%20active%20matter/ramin/prl14_activemolecules.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">this paper</a>): B particles are like little <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Missile_guidance#GOT_systems" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">homing missiles</a> that target A particles.</p><p>An example is a particle that catalyzes the reaction 2H<sub>2</sub>O<sub>2</sub> → 2H<sub>2</sub>O + O<sub>2</sub>, creating an O<sub>2</sub> gradient and interacting with it. Another example is a particle that facilitates the polymerization of a biopolymer (e.g. actin), which creates a gradient because individual monomers diffuse, whereas the polymers do not. The latter process is one possible mechanism for the propulsion of Lysteria bacteria by means of actin 'comet tails'.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sas.upenn.edu/~tidema/research.html" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">http://www.sas.upenn.edu/~tidema/research.html</a></p><p>–<a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="file:///home/guillefix/Dropbox/Oxford/MMathPhys/topics%20in%20soft%20and%20active%20matter/ramin/prl05_motor.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Propulsion of a Molecular Machine by Asymmetric Distribution of Reaction Products</a>–</p><p><sub><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevLett.94.220801" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Propulsion of a Molecular Machine by Asymmetric Distribution of Reaction Products (article)</a></sub></p><p>&quot;For a totally impermeable particle, depletion of the molecules near its surface causes a lateral slip velocity that results in net motion of the sphere. &quot;. Depletion only if the mobility is positive, which corresponds to the surface of the particle repelling the solvent molecules thus depleting</p><p>The diffusiophoretic effects also turn out to contribute to the <b>diffusion</b> of the particle (the induced velocities have a random component), with a diffusion constant that can be estimated.</p><p>Consideration of rotational diffusion is important, as it determines the time scale over which the particle is able to move consistently in a given direction.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external"

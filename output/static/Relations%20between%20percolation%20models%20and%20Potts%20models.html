<p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Percolation%20theory">Percolation theory</a></p><p>In 1969, Fortuin and Kasteleyn (FK) [27,28,103,104] found an interesting mapping between the q-state Potts model, which includes the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Ising%20model">Ising model</a> for q = 2, and a correlated bond-percolation model called the random-cluster model. <sub>It can be shown that there is a one-to-one correspondence between different thermodynamic quantities and their geometric counterparts based on the statistical and fractal properties of FK clusters.</sub></p><p>This allowed powerful renormalization group ideas to be used [74]. </p><p>Swendsen and Wang [105], and then Wolff [106], have exploited this mapping to devise extraordinar- ily efficient Monte Carlo algorithms.</p><p>There are mappings between the Ising model at a given dimension and a model of manifolds surrounding the geometric spin clusters.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://link.springer.com/article/10.1007%2FBF01014303" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Percolation and the Potts model</a>. Many of the tools of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Statistical%20physics">Statistical physics</a> have been applied to percolation through these mappings.</p>

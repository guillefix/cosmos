<p>An <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Organic%20compound">Organic compound</a> that contains at least one cyclic (ring-shaped), planar (flat) <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Moiety">Moiety</a> with a ring of resonance bonds (most often a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Conjugated%20system">Conjugated system</a>) that exhibits more stability than other geometric or connective arrangements with the same set of atoms. Aromatic moieties are very stable and do not easily break apart and react with other substances.</p><p>The most common of these rings is the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Benzene">Benzene</a> ring.</p><p>Existence of such a stable <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Resonance%20(Chemistry)">resonant</a> cyclic can often be determined using <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Huckel's%20rule">Huckel's rule</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=RaBI3lAACKg" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Aromatic Compounds and Huckel's Rule</a></p><p>There is a class of resonant rings that are very unstable, known as <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Antiaromatic%20compound">Antiaromatic compound</a>s. Finally there are resonant rings which are non-aromatic (neither aromatic nor antiaromatic).</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eK8S51L8juo" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Aromatic, Antiaromatic, or Nonaromatic: How To Tell The Difference</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=vAW9t5uBr08" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Aromatic Heterocycles I</a></p>

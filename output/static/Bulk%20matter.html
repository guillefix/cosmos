class="tc-tags-wrapper"><span class="tc-tag-list-item"> <span class="tc-tag-label tc-btn-invisible" draggable="true" style="background-color:; fill:#333333; color:#333333;"> Matter </span> <span class="tc-drop-down tc-reveal" hidden="true"></span> </span> <span class="tc-tag-list-item"> <span class="tc-tag-label tc-btn-invisible" draggable="true" style="background-color:; fill:#333333; color:#333333;"> Mechanics </span> <span class="tc-drop-down tc-reveal" hidden="true"></span> </span> </div> </div> <div class="tc-tiddler-body tc-reveal"><p><strong>Bulk matter</strong> refers to a piece of <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter#Based_on_quarks_and_leptons" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">matter</a> composed of sufficiently many building blocks (elementary particles, atoms, molecules, ...) in such a way that <u>a simple <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Statistical%2520physics.html">statistical</a> description is appropriate</u>, an bulk properties like temperature, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Viscosity%2520and%2520elasticity.html">Viscosity and elasticity</a>, etc can be defined. These are also called <em>material properties</em> or <em>macroscopic properties</em>.</p><p>Bulk matter can be either composed of a single <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Phase%2520transition.html">phase</a> or a mixture of phases; see for instance <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Dispersion%2520(Chemistry).html">dispersions</a>.</p><p>I use the term &quot;form&quot; of matter to refer to a particular type of bulk matter: either a single phase or a mixture of phases. I also use the world <em>material</em> (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Materials%2520science.html">Materials science</a>) mostly to refer to a particular form of matter. I think that &quot;phase&quot; is sometimes used more widely in the same sense as I use the term &quot;form&quot;.</p><p><u>When is a system bulk matter?</u></p><p>Note that many pieces of matter are formed by components interacting in complicated ways, in such a way that a simple statistical description does not appropriately describe its behaviour for many purposes; for instance, a computer, or a cell. These are in most situations not considered as bulk matter, and should be treated as <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Complex%2520systems.html">Complex systems</a> instead. However, whether a statistical description is appropriate, and therefore whether they are considered bulk matter, really depends on the problem, and so for some problems, these systems can be considered bulk matter (for instance, when studying the overall mechanical strength of a computer system). From here on, &quot;matter&quot; refers to bulk matter, unless otherwise specified. </p><h2 class=""><u>Classification</u></h2><p>Bulk matter can be classified depending on whether the phase (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Condensed%2520matter%2520physics.html">Condensed matter physics</a> for more detailed explanation):</p><ul><li>is <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Condensed%2520matter%2520physics.html">condensed or non-condensed</a>.</li><li>is a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Fluid%2520mechanics.html">fluid</a>, a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solid%2520mechanics.html">solid</a>, or a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Viscoelasticity.html">viscoelastic material</a>, depending on its <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Viscosity%2520and%2520elasticity.html">elasticity</a> properties. Fluids flow easily, while solids do not.</li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solid-state%2520physics.html">Hard</a> vs <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Soft%2520matter%2520physics.html">soft</a>, depending on whether the internal degrees of freedom of the material are affected significantly by thermal fluctuations (soft), or not (hard).</li></ul><h2 class=""><u>Physics</u></h2><h3 class=""><u>Mechanics</u></h3><p>The <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Mechanics.html">Mechanics</a> of most classical types of bulk matter can be macroscopically described via <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Continuum%2520mechanics.html">Continuum mechanics</a>, which <u>describes matter in terms of <em>continuum equations</em></u>, based on space-time varying <em>fields</em> that evolve according to <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Differential%2520equations.html">Differential equations</a>.</p><p>This is the foundational theory used in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Mechanical%2520engineering.html">Mechanical engineering</a>, and related areas. Continuum equations are also widely used in physics, particularly in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solid%2520mechanics.html">Solid mechanics</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Fluid%2520mechanics.html">Fluid mechanics</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Soft%2520matter%2520physics.html">Soft matter physics</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Astrophysics.html">Astrophysics</a>, rheology, etc.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Rheology.html">Rheology</a> is a branch of continuum mechanics that <u>studies the flow of matter</u>, primarily in a liquid state, but also as 'soft solids' or solids under conditions in which they respond with plastic flow rather than deforming elastically in response to an applied force. That is, rheology does not study a particular class of bulk matter, but the flow of any bulk matter.</p><h3 class=""><u>Thermodynamics</u></h3><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Thermodynamics.html">Thermodynamics</a> is the classical theory describing the flow of heat through matter. It is often combined with continuum mechanics to explain phenomena such as convection.</p><h3 class=""><u>Modern physics</u>: statistical physics, quantum mechanics</h3><p>There are many more complex phases of matter particularly in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Soft%2520matter%2520physics.html">soft condensed matter</a>, that go beyond those simply described by continuum equations (although these are still very useful in many of these). Description of these often needs more advanced ideas from <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Statistical%2520physics.html">Statistical physics</a>.</p><p>The microscopic study of matter, as done for example in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Quantum%2520condensed%2520matter%2520physics.html">Quantum condensed matter physics</a> and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Statistical%2520physics.html">Statistical physics</a>, also goes beyond phenomenological and macroscopic descriptions of classical physics and tries to derive materials properties from microscopic physics.</p></div> </div> </p> </section> </body> </html>

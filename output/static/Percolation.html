guillefix </a> 4th November 2016 at 9:43am </div> </div> <div class=" tc-reveal"> <div class="tc-tags-wrapper"></div> </div> <div class="tc-tiddler-body tc-reveal"><p>Generally, <strong>percolation</strong> refers to qualitative changes in connectivity in systems (specially large ones) as its components are added or removed. In particular, percolation most often refers to the case where a system goes from being &quot;mostly disconnected&quot; to &quot;mostly connected&quot;, in some sense. A more general mathematical model inspired by percolation and the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Potts%2520model.html">Potts model</a> is the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Random-cluster%2520model.html">Random-cluster model</a>.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percolation_theory" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Percolation theory</a>, from the perspective of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Network%2520theory.html">Network theory</a> describes the behavior of connected clusters in a network (often modelled as a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Random%2520graph.html">random graph</a>), as some substructures in the network are added or removed. The most common types are random site and bond percolation, where one removes either nodes or edges with a uniform probability, known as the <strong>occupation probability</strong>. However, there are other types (see below). </p><p>Again, from the perspective of networks, the transition from the system being &quot;disconnected&quot; to &quot;connected&quot;, is most often made precise by the appearance of a <strong>giant connected component</strong>. See below.</p><p><sub>Often, the theory of percolation is concerned with the clustering properties of identical objects which are randomly and uniformly distributed through space with a given occupation probability. However, these uniformity assumptions may be relaxed in other types of percolation.</sub></p><p>Keywords: <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Network%2520science.html">Network science</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Complex%2520systems.html">Complex systems</a>.</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/WyFMY2q.png"> from <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0370157315002008" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">here</a></p><h3 class=""><u>References</u></h3><p>Newman's book, and Mason and Gleeson tutorial have good reviews. See more at <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="References%2520for%2520percolation.html">References for percolation</a></p><h2 class=""><u><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Percolation%2520theory.html">Percolation theory</a></u></h2><p>Mathematical theory of percolation, with several important results, and discoveries.</p><h2 class=""><u>Percolation <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Phase%2520transition.html">phase transition</a></u></h2><h3 class=""><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Percolation%2520phase%2520transition.html">Percolation phase transition</a></h3><p>A phase transition occurs between a phase without a <strong>giant connected component</strong> and a phase with one. A giant connected component, or GCC, is a connected component that contains a finite fraction of the nodes as the network size <span><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>N</mi><mo>→</mo><mi mathvariant="normal">∞</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">N \rightarrow \infty</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="strut" style="height:0.68333em;"></span><span class="strut bottom" style="height:0.68333em;vertical-align:0em;"></span><span class="base textstyle uncramped"><span class="mord mathit" style="margin-right:0.10903em;">N</span><span class="mrel">→</span><span class="mord mathrm">∞</span></span></span></span></span>, i.e. it has an &quot;extensive&quot; scaling. The transition occurs at a critical value of the occupation probability, known as the <strong>percolation threshold</strong>.</p><h3 class=""><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Critical%2520phenomena%2520in%2520percolation.html">Critical phenomena in percolation</a></h3><h2 class=""><u><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Types%2520of%2520percolation%2520models.html">Types of percolation models</a></u></h2><p>Main types:</p><ul><li>Site percolation</li><li>Bond percolation</li><li>K-core percolation</li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Explosive%2520percolation.html">Explosive percolation</a></li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Bootstrap%2520percolation.html">Bootstrap percolation</a></li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Directed%2520percolation.html">Directed percolation</a></li><li>Limited path percolation</li><li>K-clique percolation</li><li>Percolation in Multilayer networks</li><li>etc.</li></ul><h2 class=""><u>Applications of percolation models</u></h2><h3 class=""><u>Applications to porous materials</u></h3><h3 class=""><u><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Application%2520of%2520percolation%2520models%2520in%2520topography.html">Applications to the study of landscapes</a></u></h3><p>Applications in topography (study of landscapes) has been found, in particular relating to:</p><ul><li>Statistical and fractal properties of watersheds.</li><li>Percolation of water bodies as water level rises in a landscape.</li></ul></div> </div> </p> </section> </body> </html>

<p>Generally, <strong>percolation</strong> refers to qualitative changes in connectivity in systems (specially large ones) as its components are added or removed. In particular, percolation most often refers to the case where a system goes from being &quot;mostly disconnected&quot; to &quot;mostly connected&quot;, in some sense. A more general mathematical model inspired by percolation and the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Potts%20model">Potts model</a> is the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Random-cluster%20model">Random-cluster model</a>.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Percolation_theory" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Percolation theory</a>, from the perspective of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Network%20theory">Network theory</a> describes the behavior of connected clusters in a network (often modelled as a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Random%20graph">random graph</a>), as some substructures in the network are added or removed. The most common types are random site and bond percolation, where one removes either nodes or edges with a uniform probability, known as the <strong>occupation probability</strong>. However, there are other types (see below). </p><p>Again, from the perspective of networks, the transition from the system being &quot;disconnected&quot; to &quot;connected&quot;, is most often made precise by the appearance of a <strong>giant connected component</strong>. See below.</p><p><sub>Often, the theory of percolation is concerned with the clustering properties of identical objects which are randomly and uniformly distributed through space with a given occupation probability. However, these uniformity assumptions may be relaxed in other types of percolation.</sub></p><p>Keywords: <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Network%20science">Network science</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Complex%20systems">Complex systems</a>.</p><p><img src="http://i.imgur.com/WyFMY2q.png"> from <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0370157315002008" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">here</a></p><h3 class=""><u>References</u></h3><p>Newman's book, and Mason and Gleeson tutorial have good reviews.

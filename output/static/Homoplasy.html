<p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Evolution">Evolution</a></p><p><strong>Homplasy</strong> is the appearance of similar traits in organisms when their most common recent ancestor didn't have them.</p><p>See <em><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convergent_evolution" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Wiki article</a></em></p><p><img src="http://rpdp.net/sciencetips_v2/images/L12D3_5.gif"></p><p>The causes of homoplasy are sometimes elaborated in the context of the difference between:</p><ul><li><strong>parallel evolution</strong>, where homoplasy is thought to occur because</li></ul><p>two organisms share a common genetic heritage, and</p><ul><li>(proper) <strong>convergent evolution</strong>, where the same solution is found by different</li></ul><p>genetic means, and where the primary causal force is usually attributed to selection</p><p>See <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1558-5646.2011.01289.x/abstract" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank"> Convergence, adaptation, and constraint</a>. This binary distinction may be too simplistic (see [36–39] for some recent discussion). For the GP map bias in the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Arrival%20of%20the%20frequent">Arrival of the frequent</a>, the reason for this repetition is not a contingent common genetic history, nor the Allmacht (german for <em>omnipotence</em>) of selection <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.worldcat.org/title/all-sufficiency-of-natural-selection-a-reply-to-herbert-spencer/oclc/37315932" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">[40]</a>, but rather a different kind of ‘deep structure in biology’ <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://www.templetonpress.org/content/deep-structure-biology" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">[41]</a>.</p>

tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Viscoelasticity.html">viscoelastic</a> <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Polymer.html">Polymer</a> (also called <em>elastomer</em>). What makes it viscoelastic is most often that the polymer is cross-linked (though not too cross-linked, as that can lead to rigid materials).</p><p>Traditionally, cross-linking was done by exposing natural latex to sulfur, a process known as vulcanization.</p><p>Although rubbers are viscoelastic, there is really a continuum between solid and viscoelastic, and some are closer to solids, with others are more clearly viscoelastic.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silly_Putty" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Silly putty</a> is interesting (apart from fun), because it has viscoelastic properties, but the polymers it's made of are not cross-linked, they are just very long!</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.open.edu/openlearn/science-maths-technology/science/chemistry/introduction-polymers/content-section-5.2.1" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">http://www.open.edu/openlearn/science-maths-technology/science/chemistry/introduction-polymers/content-section-5.2.1</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://arxiv.org/pdf/1103.5768.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Viscoelastic Behavior of Rubbery Materials</a></p><p><u>Glass transition temperature</u></p><p>There is a temperature, called the glass transition temperature, below which a cross-linked polymer stops being viscoelastic (and thus a rubber), and becomes glassy, and hard.</p><p>Above the glass transition temperature, the polymer chains are loose and floppy, and that's why a rubber classifies as a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Soft%2520matter%2520physics.html">soft material</a>. </p><p>Rubbers are also <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoplastic" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">thermoplastic</a>.</p><p><a

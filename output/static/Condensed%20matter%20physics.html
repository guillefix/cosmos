Physics </span> <span class="tc-drop-down tc-reveal" hidden="true"></span> </span> </div> </div> <div class="tc-tiddler-body tc-reveal"><p><strong>Condensed matter physics</strong> is the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Physics.html">Physics</a> of condensed matter. Below we look at the different broad types of condensed matter. The properties of condensed matter systems depend, among other things, on the chemical composition of the system (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Chemistry.html">Chemistry</a>), and the <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Physics.html">physical laws</a> the chemical components obey.</p><h3 class=""><u>Condensed vs non-condensed</u></h3><p><strong>Condensed matter</strong> refers to <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Bulk%2520matter.html">Bulk matter</a> in a condensed form, i.e. one that is composed of condensed phases. Condensed phases include mainly <strong><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solid.html">Solid</a></strong>, and <strong><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Liquid.html">Liquid</a></strong>. However, generally, it is a phase for which the particles adhere to each other strongly enough (by for example <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Intermolecular%2520forces.html">Intermolecular forces</a> or <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Chemical%2520bonds.html">Chemical bonds</a>) relative to their kinetic energy so that the system remains approximately bound in the absence of external forces, or where the particles are sufficiently highly concentrated so that they interact strongly (for example non-attracting particles can be forced to condense by confining them in a small volume (or by some external force, like gravity), forcing them to be &quot;nearly touching&quot;, as in a liquid or solid).</p><p><strong><em>Non-condensed</em> matter</strong> has constituents that are barely bound together, if at all, and thus often need to be confined, either naturally, or artificially, to be studied as a whole. The main types are: <b><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Gas.html">Gas</a>es</b> (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Fluid%2520mechanics.html">Fluid mechanics</a>), and <b><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Plasma%2520physics.html">plasmas</a></b>.</p><h3 class=""><u>Solid vs fluid</u></h3><p>A <strong><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solid.html">Solid</a></strong> is a form of matter that can resist a considerable amount of stress without flowing (so that its only response is <em>elastic</em>).</p><p>A <strong><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Fluid.html">Fluid</a></strong> is a form of matter that flows under virtually any amount of stress.</p><p>A <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Viscoelasticity.html">viscoelastic material</a> displays solid-like elasticity of short time scales, and fluid-like viscosity on long time scales.</p><p>There is really a continuum between these. For instance some <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Rubber.html">Rubber</a>s are closer to solids, while others are more clearly viscoelastic, depending on the ratio of elastic to viscous <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">deformation</a>.</p><h3 class=""><u>Hard vs soft</u></h3><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solid-state%2520physics.html">Solid-state physics</a> studies matter in <strong>hard form</strong>. Hard forms are characterized by strong inter-particle bonding (often <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Chemical%2520bonds.html">Chemical bonds</a>, when at room temperature). This bonding is strong enough that it makes the relative position of the particles essentially fixed, with thermal fluctuations making particles vibrate only a bit relative to this fixed position. It is also strong enough to resist relatively large external stresses (i.e. it doesn't flow) All forms of hard matter are solids.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Soft%2520matter%2520physics.html">Soft matter physics</a> studies matter in other condensed forms (<strong>soft forms</strong>), where some or all (relative) positional degrees of freedom are &quot;soft&quot;, that is, strongly affected by thermal fluctuations, so that they have large variances. It also includes forms with weak bonding so that the material can't resist barely any external stress without flowing. Soft matter can be a solid or a fluid.</p><p>Note that given the definition above, one expects an spectrum between the two types of matter, as the definitions involve quantities that can potentially take a continuous of values. Most materials in nature, however, can be classified in one or the other.</p><p>One of the most important properties of materials is that they exhibit different <strong>phases</strong>. These are understood through the study of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Phase%2520transition.html">Phase transition</a>s. See Paul and Lubenski's book <em>Principles of condensed matter physics</em>.</p><h3 class=""><u>Condensed forms of matter</u></h3><p><em>Hard forms</em></p><ul><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallinity" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Crystalline</a> solids (crystalline symmetry). See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solid-state%2520physics.html">Solid-state physics</a>.</li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorphous_solid" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Amorphous solids</a>; in particular glasses (disordered; statistically homogeneous/isotropic, but positions fixed). See discusion in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Viscosity%2520and%2520elasticity.html">Viscosity and elasticity</a>.</li><li>Nuclear matter.</li><li>Porous solids (see <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Porous%2520material.html">Porous material</a>).</li><li>Many <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Polymer.html">polymers</a>, particularly plastics, are solids.</li></ul><p><em><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Soft%2520materials.html">Soft forms</a></em></p><ul><li>Simple liquids (fully homogeneous/isotropic). See <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Fluid%2520mechanics.html">Fluid mechanics</a>. The fluid may be a <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Solution%2520(Chemistry).html">solution</a>, but that only changes it <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Chemistry.html">chemical</a> properties, not its physical ones.</li><li>Several types of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Complex%2520fluid%2520dynamics.html">complex fluids</a>. Main types:<ul><li>Solid–liquid (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Suspension.html">Suspension</a>s (particularly <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Colloid.html">Colloid</a>s) and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Gels.html">Gels</a>)</li><li>solid–gas (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Granular%2520material.html">Granular material</a>s, and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Fibrous%2520material.html">Fibrous material</a>s)</li><li>liquid–gas (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Foam.html">Foam</a>s)</li><li>liquid–liquid (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Emulsion.html">Emulsion</a>s)</li></ul></li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Liquid%2520crystals.html">Liquid crystals</a>: nematic, smectic, cholesteric, columnar, etc.</li><li>Several <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Polymer.html">polymeric</a> materials are soft. E.g. some <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Plastic.html">Plastic</a>s, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Rubber.html">Rubber</a>s, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Latex.html">Latex</a></li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Surfactant.html">Surfactant</a>s</li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Biological%2520matter.html">Biological matter</a></li><li>Many kinds of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Active%2520matter.html">Active matter</a> and <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Driven%2520matter.html">Driven matter</a>.</li><li><a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Pastes.html">Pastes</a>, or <a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://journals.aps.org/prl/abstract/10.1103/PhysRevLett.78.2020" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">soft glassy materials</a></li></ul><h3 class=""><u>Non-condensed forms of matter</u></h3><ul><li>simple <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Gas.html">Gas</a>es</li><li>Simple <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Plasma%2520physics.html">plasmas</a>.</li><li>Quark-gluon plasmas</li><li>Some types of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Complex%2520fluid%2520dynamics.html">complex fluids</a> are non-condensed phases, for instance <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Aerosol.html">Aerosol</a>s.</li></ul><p>There are also phases of matters that exhibit quantum effects. These are studied (along with other non-quantum phases that nontheless can be understood using quantum mechanics) in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Quantum%2520condensed%2520matter%2520physics.html">Quantum condensed matter physics</a></p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_and_disorder_(physics)" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Order and disorder</a> designate the presence or absence of some symmetry or correlation in a many-particle system.

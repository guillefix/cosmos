See below.</p><p><u>Thermal interactions</u></p><p>via self-generated temperature gradient (via half-coating of a dark absorbing material and laser radiation bathing the sample), and the Soret effect, also known as <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Thermophoresis.html">Thermophoresis</a></p><p><u>Fokker-Planck description</u></p><p><u>Regimes</u></p><p>Depend on the Soret number</p><hr><h3 class=""><u><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="file:///home/guillefix/Dropbox/Oxford/MMathPhys/topics%20in%20soft%20and%20active%20matter/ramin/prl14_colloidalswarm.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Emergent Cometlike Swarming of Optically Driven Thermally Active Colloids</a></u></h3><p><u>Brownian dynamics simulation</u> of <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Self-thermophoresis.html">self-thermophoretic</a> colloids. The colloids don't have an intrinsic asymmetry, but there is an asymmetry in their produced temperature fields because of non-uniform illumination of the light-absorbing (dark) colloid. The illumination is assumed to be directed from above downwards, and the effects of shadowing by the colloids above a particular colloid are taken into account using simple geometric optics (as a better optics treatment using light scattering on the particles is computationally very intensive). </p><p><u>Comet-like swarms are formed</u>, with interesting dynamic features, like internal circulation of particles in the swarm, evaporation, and ejection of hot particles from the tip. The high-density head region forms a hot core which pulls the tail of the comet along.</p><p>It also drives <u>thermal and density fluctuations</u>.

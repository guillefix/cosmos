<p>Mathematics is the study of <strong>structures</strong> themselves. These are necessary in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Science">Science</a> and in <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Art">Art</a>, as both require the invention of structures to either explain (and thus <em>understand</em>) the world, or for any other purpose (in the case of Art).</p><p>Mathematics, however, doesn't concern itself with the purposes or details of particular structures; rather, it commits itself with the abstract properties common among many structures. </p><p>Math is just what happens when one decides to think logically. It is the study of the logical consequences of arbitrary axioms.</p><p>It is both the Art and Science of the <strong>structure of structures</strong>. It studies many structures in the world, and creates an abstract structure to understand them. In this sense it is a Science. It also creates new unobserved abstract structures, often, generalizing observed ones. In this sense it is an Art.</p><h3 class=""><u>Common structures</u></h3><ul><li>One can have things and sets of things (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Set%20theory">Set theory</a>)</li><li>Relations among things<ul><li>One can get new things by putting together old things, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Abstract%20algebra">Abstract algebra</a></li><li>or things may just have some given relations (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Graph%20theory">Graph theory</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Network%20theory">Network theory</a>)</li></ul></li><li>One can count things (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Number%20theory">Number theory</a>)</li><li>One can have many, many things (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Analysis">Analysis</a>). For example, it seems like there is an <em>infinity</em> of instants in every second..<ul><li>Can we define things that make sense, as the number of things keeps growing? <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Limits%20and%20infinity">Limits and infinity</a><ul><li>Can we still add and subtract these things? If yes, -&gt; <em>Differential stuff</em>! (e.g. <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Differential%20equations">Differential equations</a>, <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Differential%20geometry">Differential geometry</a>)</li></ul></li></ul></li><li>Things may change, for example, in time<ul><li>Things may undergo processes, that can be composed, one after the other. <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Category%20theory">Category theory</a></li><li>Things may evolve according to some given rule: if you're here, go there. <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Dynamical%20system">Dynamical system</a></li></ul></li><li>Things can have shape (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Geometry">Geometry</a>)<ul><li>Things can have a shape, made out of many, many flat pieces. If done right, we get <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Differential%20geometry">Differential geometry</a></li><li>Can we get shapes by combining things in certain ways? <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Algebraic%20geometry">Algebraic geometry</a></li><li>Some shapes are more similar than others (<a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Topology">Topology</a>)<ul><li>Can we compare shapes by subdividing a shape in many (possibly many <big><em>many</em></big>) parts, and looking at how these parts relate and overlap? <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="#Point-set%20topology">Point-set topology</a></li><li>Can one combine shapes? <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves"

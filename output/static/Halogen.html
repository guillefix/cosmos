</span> </div> </div> <div class="tc-tiddler-body tc-reveal"><p>The Group 7 elements are known as the halogens. They are reactive non-metals and are always found in compounds with other elements. Chlorine, bromine and iodine are all halogens.</p><p>Group 7 elements form salts when they react with metals. The term ‘halogen’ means 'salt former'.</p><h2 class=""><u>Physical properties</u></h2><h3 class=""><u>Melting and boiling points</u></h3><p>Halogen molecules (which are diatomic), are attracted by <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Van%2520der%2520Waals%2520force.html">Van der Waals force</a>s, which are rather weak. As we move down the group, however, the molecules become heavier, and the vdW force becomes stronger, and so the melting and boiling points increase.</p><p><img src="http://image.slidesharecdn.com/chemmattersch7covalentbonding-120712101450-phpapp01/95/chem-matters-ch7covalentbonding-28-728.jpg?cb=1342088275"></p><p><img src="http://a.files.bbci.co.uk/bam/live/content/zvktvcw/large"></p><h3 class=""><u>Color</u></h3><p>The halogens become darker as you go down the group. Fluorine is very pale yellow, chlorine is yellow-green, and bromine is red-brown. Iodine crystals are shiny purple - but easily turn into a dark purple vapour when they are warmed up.</p><p><a class="tc-tiddlylink-external" href="http://www.bbc.co.uk/education/guides/z3vwxnb/revision/2" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">http://www.bbc.co.uk/education/guides/z3vwxnb/revision/2</a></p><h2 class=""><u>Reactivity of halogens</u></h2><p>The non-metal elements in Group 7 - known as the halogens - get less reactive as you go down the group, as the atoms attract less strongly (and their reactions depend on them gaining an electron, unlike typical reactions with <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Alkali%2520metal.html">Alkali metal</a>s)</p><h2 class=""><u>Halogen <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Single%2520replacement%2520reaction.html">displacement reactions</a></u></h2><p>This type of reaction happens with all the halogens. A more reactive halogen displaces a less reactive halogen from a solution of one of its salts.</p><p>Halogen displacement reactions are <a class="tc-tiddlylink tc-tiddlylink-resolves" href="Redox%2520reaction.html">Redox reaction</a>s because the halogens gain electrons and the halide ions lose electrons.</p><p>When we consider one of the displacement reactions, we can see which element is being oxidised and which is being reduced.</p><p>bromine + potassium iodide → iodine + potassium bromide</p><p>Br<sub>2</sub> + 2KI → I<sub>2</sub> + 2KBr As an ionic equation (ignoring the ‘spectator’ potassium ions): Br<sub>2</sub> + 2I- → I<sub>2</sub> + 2Br-</p><p>We can see that the bromine has gained electrons, so it has been reduced. The iodide ions have lost electrons, so they have been oxidised.</p></div> </div> </p> </section> </body> </html>
